Professional Bio



Background

I was born and raised in Milwaukee, WI. When I was young I aspired to pursue higher education, but like many others wanting specific academic interests. Soccer was always a passion of mine, so finding a University supportive of my academic goals and athletic desires was important to me. The University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee (UWM) was the place for me.


Undergrad

When I was growing up, mental illness was not a stranger. One of my close friends, and several relatives were impacted by mental illness. These experiences provided me with real-world perspective, and triggered my interests in human behavior, the mind, and eventually the brain. When I arrived at UWM, my undergraduate studies were broadly distributed because I was unsure about what would evolve into my area of study. For much of my undergrad, I worked in a local law firm in downtown Milwaukee advancing my professional experiences while being introduced to law as I entertained the idea of law school after college. Cue Psychology; my experience in UWM’s undergraduate program in psychology centered my preexisting interests in mental health toward topics of cognition and neuroscience. Admission into Professor Deborah Hannula’s MINDfull Memory research lab, and collaboration with fellow lab members routed these interests into a sharpened scope of study. Perhaps not unexpectedly, my plans for post-graduation were muddled; I had difficulty deciding between law school and graduate school.


After I completed my bachelors in psychology at UWM in December of 2015, I took six months off from school – working as a firm administrator at the same law firm - to find a fitting post-baccalaureate position as a professional research assistant. In July of 2016, I was pleased to assume the position of Clinical Research Associate working with Dr. David Warren, Ph.D. at the University of Nebraska Medical Center (UNMC)


What's Next?

Under the mentorship of Dr. Warren, I've been enhancing my research experience using a variety of cognitive neuroscience methods including neuroimaging, eye-tracking, and neuropsychological tests. I believe that each of my research experiences thus far has blended, giving framework for a successful career in cognitive neuroscience. Until my next big step, I carry on working toward graduate program application. More to come!
